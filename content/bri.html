Introduction

The Kpwe are among the less numerous peoples of Cameroon, a diverse African republic with more than two hundred ethnic groups. For centuries, they have inhabited a few dozen villages at the foot of Mount Cameroon, just north of the Equator. Today, they number less than thirty thousand.

Most Kpwe are now at least nominally Christian, but their practice of holding day-long funerals continues, only with the addition of Christian elements such as the participation of church choirs. Kpwe funerals are costly and extensive affairs, involving troupes of professional dancers and the singing of poetic dirges all throughout the day. These dirges feature a call-and-response structure, either between the Lead Singer (LS) and the Chorus (Ch) or between two performers.

An analysis of such dirges (Mutia 2003) appears to be the only major work on Kpwe poetry. This page's discussion draws almost entirely on it.


Organizing devices

There is no fixed meter or rhyme in Kpwe funeral dirges. Alliteration is common but does not appear to be systematic.

The main means by which Kpwe funeral songs are organized is instead parallelism. Paired words occur, but repetition is far more common—indeed, almost defining of Kpwe poetry. Dirge No. 13 features examples of paired words (bolded) and pervasive repetition of both words and lines:

LS: Likεli e likεli lingi lina

    Likεli e e

    Likεli e likεli lingi lina

    Likεli e e

    Okalata walana

    Okalata wunyana

Ch: Likεli e e

LS: O kalata hweohza e

Ch: Likεli e e

LS: O kalata litumba e

Ch: Likεli e e

LS: That name has been struck off

    It has been struck off

    That name has been struck off

    It has been struck off

    From the list of women

    From the list of men

Ch: It has been struck off

LS: From the list of friends

Ch: It has been struck off

LS: From the list of family

Ch: It has been struck off

A single performer or lead singer may repeat his lines, as seen in Dirge No. 10, sung by a single performer.

E gbwe ya nε hweyahze a nu hzee

  E gbwe ya nε hweyahze a nu hzee

Hwelingε hwianu hwelu njuma

  Hwelingε hwianu hwelu njuma

Do all you can on earth

  Do all you can on earth

Remember judgment awaits your souls

  Remember judgment awaits your souls

The performer may repeat his lines in an extended chiasm, when the order of elements is reversed, as in Dirge No. 3:

wangɔ wa ndingε malingo

mɔndi jo janeli,

  tatε iwondε;

    tombihzε maa o mo wowe

      mɔ aku na ihzɔ

      aku na ihzɔ

    tombihzε maa o mo wowe,

  tatε iwondε;

wangɔ wa ndingε malingo

mɔndi jo janeli.

The one you loved so much

is the one we have brought to you,

  Father of Creation;

    Extend your hands and receive him

      For he no longer dwells with us

      He no longer dwells with us

    Extend your hands and receive him,

  Father of Creation;

The one you loved so much

is the one we have brought to you.

The chorus may simply repeat the lead singer's lines, as seen in Dirge No. 1:

LS: Ya ajulεnε ì ndutu ja anyu é

    kaka mwema

    Ya ajulεnε ì ndutu ja anyu é

    kaka mwema

    Nanu ndi e hze ehweli

Ch: Nanu ndi e hze ehweli

LS: Nanu ndi e hze ehweli

Ch: Nanu ndi e hze ehweli

LS: Wenga kaka mwema

LS: Persevere with your sorrow

    take heart

    Persevere with your sorrow

    take heart

    That's how the world is

Ch: That's how the world is

LS: That's how the world is

Ch: That's how the world is

LS: So take heart

Or the chorus may echo and develop on the last line of the lead singer in a call-and-response format, as with ihfahzu "Almighty" in Dirge No. 5:

LS: Owa ohzi hwiye mama eja o njia

    Wenga i wεlε ihfahzu

Ch: Ihfahzu o ihfahzu o ja

    O wεlε tatε

    Inyɔhfε e jae e jo ongoane

LS: You don’t know what will befall you

    So let’s call on the Almighty

Ch: We call on the Almighty

    We call on our ancestors

    To come and help us

Finally, interjections of grief, such as wai, o, and e, are extremely common in Kpwe funeral poetry and sometimes make up entire lines.

Dirge No. 11 is noteworthy for its use of interjections. The song consists entirely of five words: Mama "Mother", eya hwa "respond", and the interjections o and e (pronounced "oh" and "eh"). By interspersing the sobs represented by o and e with only the call for an absent Mother and the unanswered demand for her response, and by progressively reducing the length of lines—as if the mourners are slowly realizing that their mother is truly and forever gone—the point that Mother is dead and will never reply is elegantly made.

LS: Mama eya hwa o e e e, eya hwa

    Mama eya hwa o e e e, eya hwa

Ch: Mama eya hwa o e e e, eya hwa

    eya hwa e e e

LS: Mama eya hwa e e e

    eya hwa e e e

Ch: Eya hwa... e e e

LS: Mother, respond, o e e e, respond

    Mother, respond, o e e e, respond

Ch: Mother respond, o e e e, respond

    respond e e e

LS: Mother respond e e e

    respond e e e

Ch: Respond... e e e
