BUGIS

  • Affiliation: Austronesian > Malayo-Polynesian > South Sulawesi > Bugis
  • Area: Five million speakers, mostly in South Sulawesi Province, Indonesia, but with diaspora communities scattered across Archipelagic Southeast Asia
  • ISO 639-3 code: bug

Introduction

Bugis is the most widely used language of Indonesia's South Sulawesi Province. Its speakers, also called the Bugis, have historically been among the most enterprising peoples of Southeast Asia. The dynasties founded by Bugis adventurers ranged over thousands of miles, from Sumatra to the Lesser Sunda Islands; even today, Bugis Street, more than a thousand miles west of the Bugis homeland, remains a favorite haunt of many Singaporeans.

The Bugis adopted writing through disputed channels in the fifteenth century, and they have been a literate people ever since. Their traditional script, the lontaraq, is an abugida, a writing system that writes only consonants as full letters and vowels as diacritics (accent marks). Most of the poetry discussed here is written in this script.

The Bugis converted to Islam in the early seventeenth century, although a very small animist minority still remains. However, even as Muslims, most Bugis continue to hold the pre-Islamic epic poem I La Galigo, one of the longest works of poetry ever created, in high regard. Besides the I La Galigo, the Bugis literary tradition includes many other forms of poetry.


Organizing devices

Bugis meter is usually strictly syllable-counting. There are three types: a five-syllable meter, very rare outside the sacred epic I La Galigo; an eight-syllable meter; and élong, a three-stanza poem with an 8-7-6 meter.

However, there is one exception to syllable-counting, called oxytonal shortening. A stressed syllable in fourth position may function as two syllables, meaning that four- or seven-syllable lines are permitted.

This is a good example of phonological archaism in poetry. On some level, poets still remember that most stressed syllables in Bugis originally developed from contractions of two syllables. For example, the stressed final syllable -é in modern Bugis developed from a historic -ia, and a final stressed -óng was originally -oang. Bugis poets have kept to an ancient five-syllable meter, one possibly even predating the fifteenth-century introduction of writing (which writes -é or -óng according to modern one-syllable pronunciation), even when it means accepting exceptions today.

The I La Galigo is written in a five-syllable meter. The average word in the I La Galigo is 2.7 syllables long, which means that one sentence of even middling length must be in multiple five-syllable lines. The I La Galigo developed strict rules as to where sentences can be cut:

  • Lines cannot cut words in half. An exception allows a noun with two roots to be separated according to its roots, but even this is rare; generally speaking, words are uncuttable. This includes all proper nouns, even place names with obvious meaning.
  • In Bugis, independent or content words tend to be clustered together and surrounded by particles on either side. For example, the I La Galigo lines translated as "He had also not yet finished pronouncing his good words" in Bugis is "tĕng lĕppĕq ada madeceng to pa", literally glossing to "not he-had-finished words good also yet". The content words ("he-had-finished words good") are called the nucleus, the rest the periphery, and the whole thing a complex. There are rules to when a complex may belong to different lines:

    • The nucleus may be cut into two lines.
    • The left-hand periphery (the periphery that comes before the nucleus) may never be spread out in two different lines, nor be in a line different from the nucleus.
    • If there is a stressed syllable in the right-hand periphery (RHP) that comes after the nucleus, the RHP may be cut into different lines, but the stressed syllable itself and at least one other syllable must fall into the second line.

This means that in the case of "tĕng lĕppĕq ada madeceng to pa", cutting the sentence into different lines at "tĕng | lĕppĕq ada madeceng to pa" isn't permitted (can't separate the left-hand periphery from the nucleus), nor is "tĕng lĕppĕq ada madeceng to | pa" (pa is not stressed, and even if it were, at least two syllables from the RHP needs to be carried over to the next line).

But theoretically, "tĕng lĕppĕq | ada madeceng to pa", "tĕng lĕppĕq ada | madeceng to pa", and "tĕng lĕppĕq ada madeceng | to pa" (to is stressed) would all be permitted.

The following excerpt from the I La Galigo portrays the hero Sawérigading as having fallen in love with his long-lost twin sister. His sister is unenthusiastic and his parents aghast, with his mother scolding him and Sawérigading replying in the lines quoted below. I La Galigo lines are separated with bars and stressed syllables provoking oxytonal shortening are in red. Bugis has no diphthongs, and something like "ei" is two syllables (e-i).

Risapā sipa | rukkusenngédé

taué lé | massélingéreng.

Apaq pajaneng | mulingérang ngi | solang tanamu.

Peppeq i matti | tomaégamu

naléléi wi | roppo lipué.

Mau sipolo | tikkā taué, | sikédé nyiliq,

tempeqding to | siparukkuseng | massélingéreng,

apaq labū i | matti lipué,

napopo reppaq | tenreq mammuti

ttudang ri Lino | lé tomaéga | nalurenngé.


Taro i maté | tomaégaé,

taro i peppeq | paqbanuaé,

naé rékkua | jaji mua ni | nawa-nawakku,

tongeng mua ni | lé manasakku.

It is forbidden to become a couple

for people who are siblings.

Obviously you would call down disaster upon your country.

The people will die,

the country destroyed.

Be it for half a day, an eyeblink,

siblings cannot become a couple,

because the country will submerge,

it will be ruined

none of the people in the world will be saved.


Let the people die,

let the villagers be exterminated,

as long as my wish comes true,

my hopes are fulfilled.

The Bugis genre of toloq is metrically marked by eight-syllable lines, and also prefers parallelistic structures. Both features are visible in the quoted excerpt of the Toloqna Musuq Boné, about the Bugis queen of Boné and the Dutch (traditionally called kompania in Bugis after the Dutch East India Company) both preparing bullets for war. With the exception of a single one-word, four-syllable line, everything is in eight syllables, and parallelism is pervasive:

Ala maressaq otaé

Ala kédéq pabbojaé

Nasigugureng tumera

Tumera ritiriq lébu

Sining parulu musuqna

Sining to ritunruanna

Batara Tungkeqna Boné

Barisiqna

Kompania Balandaé.

Faster than betel can be chewed,

In the twinkling of an eye,

Lead was discharged

Round-cast lead,

By all the commanders

By all the followers

Of the Sole Queen of Boné

By the army

Of the Dutch Company.

Toloqna Musuq Boné, Daéng ri Aja, 1860.

Finally, the genre of élong involves sung stanzas of three lines with a decreasing number of syllables per line. The classical élong meter is, as mentioned, 8-7-6. I have revised the translations somewhat to fit the meter:

Peddiq-paro ko tobiu:

Malasa tenrijampang,

Maté tenriwalung.


Riwalung-si daung loka;

Rilemmeq gangka uttuq,

Nasungkeq-si bawi.

Difficult to be an orphan—

Sick and nobody to care,

Dead and with not a shroud.


Or your shroud is banana leaves;

Buried so deep as a knee,

Pigs dig you out again.

Poetic genres

The I La Galigo

The I La Galigo (ILG) is one of both the greatest and the least-known masterpieces of world literature.

Written in an episodic format using a steady five-syllable meter, the ILG extols the exploits of the first generations of gods and humans on earth, as recalled by the hero La Galigo. Although there is no comprehensive version of the work, if all the varying episodes that make up the epic were somehow put together, the poem would probably be the longest in world history, clocking at nearly five times the length of the entire Bible (and several tens of thousands of lines longer than the Sanskrit Mahabharata, usually called the world's longest epic poem). However, and despite the fact that the Bugis themselves perceive ILG as a single work, the poem is dispersed into thousands of manuscripts, mostly in private possession, ranging from a few lines at most to at least a hundred pages but never covering more than a few episodes of the vast epic. Even the longest single edition, a 2,851-page, twelve-volume version compiled in the late ninteenth century, comprises no more than a small fraction of the diversity of the ILG.

However, the ILG manuscripts remain astonishingly consistent, and it is difficult to argue that the manuscripts do not together make up a single poetic work. There is little variation in meter—the five-syllable-counting is everywhere abided—and Koolhof 1999 remarks that even the countless manuscripts "are firmly interconnected. The homogeneity of the extant manuscripts is remarkable, and the various episodes... fit like the pieces of a jigsaw puzzle. The family relationship of the protagonists in their hundreds is consistent throughout, and for most episodes the chronology is fairly easy to determine."

The ILG is in many ways a text intentionally made to sound ancient. It is written in an archaic register of Bugis that includes many expressions now obsolete or perhaps never used in the spoken language and also eschews loanwords from Malay, Arabic, and European languages that are now inseparable from the language, such as sorodadu for "soldier" (Dutch soldaat) or bandéra for "flag" (Portuguese bandeira).

The Toloq

Toloq refers to eight-syllable-line narrative poems that cover wildly varying topics: epic history, love stories, erotica, satire. The majority, however, are heroic poems discussing historical events.

Heroic toloq have a number of narrative conventions that must be adhered to:

  • Certain poetic words and metaphors (the so-called "poetic language") must be used. One very common refrain in the genre is Makkedi la ménrurana "So spoke the sovereign." Others include Ala maressaq otaé / Ala kédéq pabbojaé "Faster than betel [a type of gum] can be chewed / In the twinkling of an eye".
  • Specific dates should not be given, only relative ones such as "three days later". Even here, there is a preference to have events occur at intervals of three, seven, or forty days, months, or years, even when this goes against the historical facts.
  • Personal names may be avoided in favor of oblique references such as opu lebbuq biritta "lord with the famous name" for the protagonist or Jiniralana Bettawé "Batavia general" for the Dutch Governor-General. Batavia was the Dutch colonial capital.
  • There must be suro scenes, in which a ruler's words to a messenger are provided, then later repeated verbatim in the text, this time spoken by the messenger.
  • There must be a mangngaruq scene, in which the commanders and heroes swear elaborate oaths of allegiance before the king as they prepare to go to war.

Of course, as is always the case in poetry, these rules are never followed to the letter. The following toloq, discussing the turbulent events of the 1810s when Napoleonic shenannigans caused the British to briefly replace the Dutch as the dominant European power in Indonesia, explicitly gives the date in the Christian calendar (the Bugis traditionally used the Christian calendar despite being Muslim):

Ri uleng pébéruari

taung sisebbu arua

ratuqna seppulo dua

lélé-tongeng-ni, la béla!

akoasanna Balandaé

ri to Anggarisiq-édé…

In the month of February

of the year one thousand

eight hundred and twelve

then truly passed, my friend!

the power of the Dutch

to those Englishmen...

The Élong

The élong is by far the most divergent form of Bugis poetry. Always sung (the word literally translates to "song") and often accompanied by violin and flute, and usually but not always a triplet of eight, seven, and finally six syllables per line, the Bugis classify the genre into at least the following categories:

  • Élong anaq-anaq: Nursery rhymes
  • Élong éjaq-éjaq: Songs sung by young lovers
  • Élong maddakkala: Plowing songs
  • Élong maliung bettuanna: Riddle songs, discussed below
  • Élong mappadicawa: Nonsense joke songs
  • Élong mapparéré: Also songs exchanged between bride and groom during weddings, but more sharply so
  • Élong massagala: Songs sung during smallpox epidemics
  • Élong osong: War songs
  • Élong padondoq-anaq: Lullabies
  • Élong pangngajaq: Songs of proverbial wisdom
  • Élong sisseng-ngisseng: Songs whose lines are courteously exchanged between bride and groom during weddings
  • Élong tomaloloq: Songs about love
  • Élong topanrita: Songs about Islam sung by Muslim clerics
  • Élong totoq: Songs about orphans, as seen above

Most élong genres do not appear very well-studied in non-Indonesian scholarship.


Riddling poetry: The lover's choice / Asking out a crush in Bugis

Among the most interesting sub-genres of élong is élong maliung bettuanna, literally "deep meaning songs". "Deep" here refers not to any philosophical depth (in which case élong pangngajaq would be "deepest") but to the fact that the song has a hidden meaning.

Élong maliung bettuanna demands close familiarity with Bugis writing, culture, and geography to be properly understood, and always requires two levels of interpretation.

Ké ga na mumaberrekkeng,

buaja buluqedé,

lompuq walennaé?

Which do you prefer,

a mountain crocodile

or river silt?

First level of interpretation: the kennings. There are two kennings (circumlocutional expressions) here: "mountain crocodile" and "river silt". A learned Bugis reader or listener would understand that a "mountain crocodile" is a tiger, while "river silt" is sand. But the song is still meaningless: "Which do you prefer, a tiger or sand?"

Second level of interpretation: the script. In Bugis, "tiger" is macang and "sand" is kessiq.

The Bugis script has a number of peculiarities:

  • Double consonants are not written. Ppa and pa are written identically.
  • Final -ng and -q are not written. Ppang and paq are written the same as ppa and pa.
  • An initial m- followed by a consonant is not written. Mpaq is written the same as ppang, paq, ppa, and pa.

The word macang "tiger" is written like this:

ᨆᨌ

But because of the principles we've just listed (double consonants and final -ng ignored), these two letters could also be pronounced macca. This word happens to mean "clever".

Similarly, the word kessiq "sand" is written:

ᨀᨙᨔᨗ

This can be read as kessing "beautiful", because final -q and final -ng are both ignored. So the poem actually means:

Which do you prefer, a smart person [macca] or a beautiful one [kessing]?

(It's not a fixed rule that the written form should make no distinction between the meaning of the kenning and the ultimate meaning, so long as there's a close phonological similarity.)

Or if you wanted to ask out your crush in a roundabout way, you could write him or her the following poem:

Gellang riwataq majjékko,

Inanréna Menreqé,

Bali ulu balé.

Copper stretched in a curve,

The food of Mandar,

The opposite of fish's head.

And he or she might respond:

Inungeng mapekkeq-pekkeq,

Balinna aseqédé,

Bali ulu balé.

A sour drink,

The opposite of above,

The opposite of fish's head.

What the hell does this mean?

First level of interpretation. Each line is a kenning. The first use of curved metal that would come to a premodern Bugis person's mind is as a fishhook, méng in Bugis. The Mandar are an Indonesian ethnic group famous for their bananas, loka in Bugis. The opposite of a fish's head is its tail, ikkoq in Bugis. The Bugis don't seem to have liked sour things very much, and the closest thing they have to a "sour drink" is tea, téng. Finally, the opposite of "above" is obviously "down", awa.

Now that the kennings have been figured out, we have the following:

Méng

Loka

Ikkoq


Téng

Awa

Ikkoq

Fishhook

Banana

Tail


Tea

Down

Tail

Wow, this makes even less sense than before.

Second level of interpretation. Bugis writing traditionally does not space words. So if we write those two lines above, we have:

ᨆᨙ   ᨒᨚ   ᨀ   ᨕᨗ   ᨀᨚ

ᨈᨙ   ᨕ   ᨓ   ᨕᨗ   ᨀᨚ

The first line could be read méng loka ikkoq "fishhook banana tail", but because there aren't spaces to tell you where to separate the words, it could also be read as méloq kaq iko, which means "I love you". Remember, double consonants, -q, and -ng don't count.

The second line, similarly, might be read téng awa ikkoq "tea down tail", but also téa waq iko, "I don't want you".

So that's how to confess your love or reject someone's confession in Bugis poetry. Now you know.
