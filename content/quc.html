(Analysis: Forms / Functions)

Bibliography

K'ICHE'

  • Affiliation: Mayan > Quichean > K'iche'
  • Area: 2.3 million speakers, mostly in Guatemala
  • ISO 639-3 code: quc (Central K'iche') / acr (Achi K'iche')

Introduction

K'iche' Mayan is among the most widely used Mayan languages today, counting more than two million speakers. Its speakers, the K'iche', make up a ninth of the Guatemalan population.

The K'iche' rose to prominence in the southern Maya highlands following the collapse of the Classic Maya. The kingdom they founded once dominated most of the highlands and ruled over more than a million people. However, the K'iche' state entered a political decline in the fifteenth century and met a brutal end at the hands of the Spanish conquistador Pedro de Alvarado, who burned the K'iche' monarch alive and had their capital city of Q'umarkaj razed. Henceforth the K'iche' were subject to foreign rule. Even after Guatemalan independence in 1821, they were excluded from the white- and mestizo-dominated elite. As late as the 1980s they were among the many Maya peoples subject to a genocide committed by the American-backed government.

The destruction the Spaniards wrought on the K'iche' was beyond appaling, to the point that not a single precolonial K'iche' book remains out of the hundreds that must once have been read. However, two poetic texts from the very early colonial period have managed to survive: the Popol Wuj (c. 1550) and the Titulo de Totonicapán (1554), both works of mythistory.

The K'iche' poetry of the Popol Wuj and the Titulo remains very similar, both phonologically and in terms of the extremely parallelistic organization, to modern K'iche' poetry: the ritual chants recited during Christian ceremonies and government appointments, and the verse lines of traditional plays such as the Rabinal Achi.

(The Rabinal Achi is actually written in a somewhat divergent dialect of K'iche' called Achi, which has its own ISO 639-3 code, but Tedlock 2003 says the two dialects are actually fully mutually intelligible, and we treat it as part of K'iche'.)

In the past few decades, K'iche' poetry has been seeing a renaissance, spearheaded by figures such as Humberto Ak'ab'al. But for good or for bad, this page focuses primarily on the traditional poetry of the Popol Wuj and the Rabinal Achi.


Organizing methods: Parallelism in the Popol Wuj and the Rabinal Achi

Meter, rhyme, and alliteration are all unsystematic, and K'iche' poetry is organized almost entirely by parallelism. We first present two long excerpts of K'iche' poetry, the first from the Popol Wuj and the other from the Rabinal Achi, then discuss the specific forms of K'iche' parallelism that the two texts exemplify.

Rabinal Achi

The Rabinal Achi is a masked play in verse performed twice every January in the town of Rabinal ("the navel of the sky and earth" to its inhabitants). It commemorates the heroic fifteenth-century prince Kaweq and his futile attempt to conquer Rabinal. Kaweq is eventually captured by the king of Rabinal and put on trial, where he concedes to all the accusations made against him and accepts that he will be sacrificed to the gods. His only request is that he be allowed five things before his death: a strong drink, fine cloth, a dance with the princess Mother of Quetzal Feathers, a game with Rabinal's eagle and jaguar warriors (the Maya, like their Aztec neighbors, wore eagle and jaguar costumes in war), and a final farewell to his homeland. All these are granted, and Kaweq is finally sacrificed.

In the first of the excerpts below, Kaweq breaks the fourth wall by laughing at the musicians who are providing the music for the play in real life, saying that they ought to use his drums and flutes. He then laments that nobody here at Rabinal is sufficiently noble to dance with him. In the second excerpt, Kaweq asks the king for a dance with Mother of Quetzal Feathers, the maiden daughter of the king who is about to sacrifice him, to mark the magnitude of his impending death. In the final excerpt, Kaweq—perhaps in a last moment of self-deprecatory humor—compares himself to a bird and a squirrel, a K'iche' difrasismo (see below) for a small and powerless thing, that died while looking for mere "morsels" (contextually Rabinal, which might be but a morsel for Kaweq's great ambitions). Kaweq then invites the eagle and jaguar warriors of Rabinal to sacrifice him to their gods, ending the play (from Tedlock 2003).

Analysis

Forms of parallelism

The most common form of K'iche' parallelism is lexical, when one word is parallel with another. This might mean that the parallel version of the first line's word is given alone, or that the entire sentence is reprised with the parallel words swapped, or anything in between:

(Lexical parallelism limited to the words themselves)

Uqajaw k’aslem

      winaqirem

The Father of life

                      of humanity

(Lexical parallelism reprising entire noun phrase; K'iche' has one word for "green" and "blue")

Ajraxa laq

Ajraxa tzel

Those of the green earth

Those of the blue sky

(Lexical parallelism reprising entire sentence)

Qi ta chib’irb’ot kaj

Qi ta chib’irb’ot ulew.

The very sky would tremble

The very earth would tremble.

There are some constraints on the order of lexical parallelisms. Generally, higher-ranked or more thematically important words must come first. For example, the very existence of the gods takes precedence over their mere words:

Saqil k’olem

Saqil tzij.

Enlightened existence

Enlightened words.

The feminine takes precedence over the masculine (as in English, where "Dad and Mom" sounds off):

Kamul Iyom

Kamul Mamom

Twice grandmother

Twice grandfather

If the poet considers two non-gender parallelisms to be of equal importance, the word with less syllables comes first. If the two words have the same number of syllables, the word with less consonants may take precedence, but this seems not to be a hard-and-fast rule.

La’ kuk

La’ tz’ikin

That squirrel

That bird

Chib’ana b’a ri ichak

Chib’ana b’a ri ipatan.

Do your duty

Do your work.

There are also grammatical constraints on lexical parallelisms. Parallel terms must have identical morphology, that is, the same prefixes and suffixes. Because K'iche' morphology is considerably more complex than in English, this often translates as entire clauses being parallel, not single words as in the original text.

The Popol Wuj features parallelism between the following three morphologically complex verbs:

Waral xchiqatz’ibaj wi

        xchiqatik’iba’ wi ojer tzij

[......]

Wa’e xchiqatz’ib’aj chupam chik uch’abal Dios

[......]

      xchiqelesaj.

Here we will write

          we will implant the Ancient Word

[......]

This we will write amid the word of Dios

[......]

      we will bring it out.

These three verbs are counted as parallelisms because they share the three verbal prefixes x- (completive aspect prefix), ch- (potential mood prefix, which is contextually close to the English future tense), and q(a)- (first-person plural ergative prefix). By contrast, a verb like xchikitzok' "They will carve" is not parallel with xchiqatz’ibaj because the subject is different, even if the meaning of "carve" is much closer to "write" (considering the Maya carved hieroglyphs into stone stelae) than "implant".

Not all K'iche' parallelisms are lexical. Sometimes whole lines repeat verbatim, like a refrain:

Areta b’a, chitzaq alaq

  Ri nunima tzaqab’alaj

    nuch’uti tzaqab’alaj

Areta puch chitzaq alaq

  Ri nuyaki su’

    nuyaki q’ojom

Areta puch chitzaq alaq

  Nu K’iche’ su’

  Nu K’iche’ q’ojom

Well sirs, if you did play

  My great instruments

  My small instruments

And sirs, if you did play

  My Mexican flute

  My Mexican slit drum

And sirs, if you did play

  My K'iche' flute

  My K'iche' slit drum

Sometimes the parallelism is semantic, based more on meaning than any grammatical structures. In the following lines from the Popol Wuj, the text is defined through parallelistic structures first with its title "the Popol Wuj", then as "light that came from beside the sea", then with an antithesis as "the account of our shadows", and finally as "the dawn of life":

Maja bi chik ilbal re Popol Wuj,

                  ilbal saq petenaq ch’aqa palo,

                      utz’ijoxik qamujibal,

                  ilbal saq k’aslem.

        where to see the light that came from beside the sea,

                      the account of our shadows,

        where to see the dawn of life.

Or parallelisms may not occur in a row at all, as is the case with alternation and chiasm. In alternating parallelism, elements occur in the order ABCA'B'C', as in this description of two creator deities in a later part of the Popol Wuj (tz'ite are divination tools; from Christenson 2007):

Are' ri mama',

  Are' aj tz'ite,

    Xpiyakok ub'i'.

Are' k'u ri ati't,

  Aj Q'ij,

  Aj B'it chi raqan,

    Xmukane ub'i'.

The Grandfather,

  The Master of the Tz'ite,

    Xpiyakok by name.

The Grandmother,

  The Mistress of Days,

  The Mistress of Shaping who is at the foot,

    Xmukane by name.

In chiasm, elements occur in the order ABCC'B'A'. In this example, Kaweq first calls on the eagle warriors, then the jaguar warriors, urges them to sacrifice him now, and reminds them that this is their duty. He then does this all again, but in reverse (think about what animals have teeth).

Functions of parallelism

The logical relation in the final difrasismo is unstated by Norman.

Besides these metaphorical couplets, K'iche' parallelisms have many functions, as categorized in Christenson 2007. First, if we exclude Christenson's category of "identical parallelism", is synonymous parallelism, in which repetition creates emphasis:

Chiyata chi b’a la chuwe

Chinkajta chi b’a chi ech la

Then make me a gift, sir

Lend me something you have, sir

The second is antithetic parallelism, when the contrast between two elements emphasizes some other thing (here, the breadth of knowledge contained in the Popol Wuj):

Ewaxib’al

Saqirib’al

[......]

Saq petenaq ch’aqa palo,

Utz’ijoxik qamujibal.

What has been hidden

What has been revealed

[......]

The light that came from beside the sea,

The account of our shadows.

The third category Christenson calls associative parallelism, when two words or phrases are parallel in a material, familar, functional, or gender relationship.

(Bodies of water: a material relationship)

Uk’u’x cho

Uk’u’x palo

Heart of the Lake

Heart of the Sea
(Things of exceptional beauty: a functional relationship)

Ri u Chuch Q’uq’

      u Chuch Raxon ri yamanim

                xteq’oqib’

        Mother of Glistening Green of jade

                          precious beads
(Things a maiden hides: a functional relationship)

K’o saom uchi’

  Majab’i saom uwach

Chinsata k’u uchi’

  Chinsata k’u uwach

  Or her face has never been shown

I must show her mouth

  I must show her face
(A gender relationship)

Kuk’ achij mun

              woman slave

Finally, we have categories that Christenson calls augmentive, causative, and epithetic. The distinctions between these three all seem a bit fuzzy, and all three resemble the Biblical Hebrew parallelism of intensification.

(Augmentative: the later line explains the importance of the first. It's mildly interesting that this is the reverse of Hebrew practice, where the first line should be abstract and the second line concrete.)

Chiqajta qawi’

Chiqajta qajolom

But we would let our hair fall forward

      we would hide our faces

(Causative: the later line is caused by the first)

Ilol re

Bisol re

He who reads it

He who ponders it

(Epithetic: the later line is an epithet of the first. Here, through a chiasm, the goddess Xmukane is called "(Twice) Grandmother" and "Protector", while the god Xpiyakok is "(Twice) Grandfather" and "Shelterer".)

Iyom

Mamom Xpiyakok

              Xmukane, ubi’ Matzanel

                  Ch’uqunel

                  kamul Iyom

                  kamul Mamom

the grandmother

        grandfather Xpiyakok

                        Xmukane, thus named Protector

                            Shelterer

                            twice Grandmother

                            twice Grandfather

The great majority of lines in K'iche' poetry are subject to parallelism. Because of this, K'iche' poets could use single non-parallelistic lines (monocolons) for effect, not unlike how T. S. Eliot intentionally does not rhyme line 134 in "Prufrock". The final line of the Popol Wuj, for example, is not parallelistic, which adds appropriate poignancy; the K'iche' are conquered, their old stories complete, their very town now called by the invaders' name:

Xere k’u ri’ mi xutzinik chi konojel K’iche’, Santa Cruz ubi’.

There is only this. All is now completed concerning K'iche', called Santa Cruz.
