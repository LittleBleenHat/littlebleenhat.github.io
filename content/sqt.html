SOQOTRI

  • Affiliation: Afro-Asiatic > Semitic > South Semitic > Modern South Arabian > Soqotri
  • Area: 70,000 speakers in Socotra Island, Yemen
  • ISO 639-3 code: sqt

Introduction

The Soqotri language is one of the few surviving Modern South Arabian languages, spoken solely on the isolated Yemeni island of Socotra.

Little is known about the history of the island. Soqotri was not traditionally written, nor was the island of their speakers a major center in local trade. Our knowledge of its history thus comes almost exclusively from foreign sources that mention it in passing. It seems to have been a somewhat frequented hub of merchants in Late Antiquity, during which its inhabitants became loosely Christian. After a brief Portuguese interlude in the early sixteenth century, the island was conquered by the Arabs, and the Socotrans soon converted to Islam, their religion today.

Even today, the island remains impoverished and most of its inhabitants live off date palms, fishing, or goat herding, three activies that feature prominently in their poetry.

Socotrans nonetheless have a proud tradition of oral literature, including poetry (temtíľo). "Poetry and song used to be a normal part of everyday life on the island, a natural way of communicating with others, be they human, animals, spirits of the dead, jinn, sorcerors, or the divine" (Morris 2013). Even so, Soqotri poetry—which demands close attention to cultural context—remains understudied and little-translated to this day.


Organizing devices

Meter

Most Soqotri poetry is organized into an "eight-beat metrical pattern" (Naumkin and Kogan 2015) of "isometric eight-syllable lines" (Kogan and Bulakh 2017). Soqotri would then have a syllable-counting meter that counts for eight units. However, I find that the actual poetic texts collected by Naumkin and Kogan in their Corpus of Soqotri Oral Literature appear to have lines with more or less than eight syllables. I have not been able to find any discussion of whether this is because Soqotri meter actually counts something other than syllables, or whether this is simply normal variation like English poets playing fast and loose with iambic pentameter.

The following, Text 21 in the Corpus, is an example of both the basic eight-syllable meter and the variation within it:

Tə́MRE MEŠəNkÉRO

Wa-ṭəy šíˀho ḷə-ḥa tə́mre

Di-məľák šəẓ̂aréno

Di-hiníyo bə-di-ḳaŝáˁyhon

Di-šóuṣ̌a bə-ŝḥaf di-ˀérhon

Dəmədə́mən bə-ˀerbéto

Wa-núbot ḷaḷ təfáḷaḥ šɔm

Wa-ḷaḷ təndɛ́yrər ḳóuṣa

Wa-gédəḥ tho séˁe ˁəyyúg

Wa-ḳáḷaˁk hə́yhən ṭad ŝémraḥ

Wa-máẓ̂aˁ dɛg wa-dɛg zénog

Wa-mən-ḥa di-góˁor énṭəf

A WONDROUS PALM

I have one palm tree here,

A wondrous green one,

Planted in di-Kasayhon,

Watered with goat’s milk,

With milk-foam settling at its roots.

It is pollinated when the sun rises

And when the sun sets, one picks the fruit.

Nine men came to visit me—

I tossed them a cluster.

One ate a bit, another took a bit with him,

And even a sick one found his appetite.

Soqotri poetry does not systematically rhyme or alliterate.


Refrains

The parallelism that exists is most often based on repetition of semantically meaningless refrains. The two longest poems of the Corpus, "Ḥimbóbe" and "Ṭawíse", both feature three- or six-syllable refrains with no apparent meaning that come between every line. We look at "Ḥimbóbe" here.

The exact meaning of ḥimbóbe appears unknown, but contextually it might be the onomatopoeic bleat of the narrating goat. In any case, whatever it might mean, the repetition of this short word interspersed with the eight-syllable content lines sets a steady rhythm to the poetic text.

Other techniques

Veiled language

Soqotri makes extensive use of a form of circumlocutional speech called di-xílíyə ("what is beneath") or di-ḥarf ("what is hidden"), which Morris 2013 loosely translates as "veiled language". The defining characteristic of veiled language is that the poem can be read in two ways, one at face value and the other the real, often sexual intention of the poet.

Veiled language is traditionally considered the epitome of Soqotri literary art, and a key trope on the island is that the stupid (the "child of dung" and the "child of coldness" in Soqotri poetic parlance) are incapable of comprehending the di-xílíyə. A child who can understand veiled language is praiseworthy, as the following poem from Morris 2013 makes clear (Morris's transliteration scheme is different from the Corpus's):

dīyə de šʌm di-ber ibrəhe

yāḳaˁ bə-di-xīlīyə

gisurk əṣɔ̄nif heyh bə-ˁayn

bər šeriyeḳ bə-di-ˀətəm beyh

di-ol iśīniš ber berġob

ber šəwək ol yāḳaˁ beyh

What a good day it was

When my grandson began to understand veiled language!

I could send him a signal with my eye,

give him a sideway glance we both understood and shared.

One that the child of dung does not see,

and the child of coldness does not understand.

For an example of veiled language in action, we'll look at two other examples from Morris 2013.

di-ho ʌḷḷʌh təˁamer ənhe

di-ho bə-ḵʌriyʌ di-ḥegəher

ber ol ḵēbaˁk di-ho żeyfə

bə-šʌm di-nibititin

Oh my lord, give me a guardian

For my date-palm plantation!

For I held nothing back but used all my strength

On the day of pollination.

The word nibititin literally means "pollination of palm trees". The direct meaning of the poem is clear:

God, my date palm plantation isn't doing well, even though I did my best to take care of the trees. Please send me a helper.

But to take this as the poem's meaning is to be a "child of dung, a child of coldness". A learned Socotran would know that there must be a veiled meaning that can be divined like a riddle. For example, who is the helper of a man's date palm farm? In Socotra, almost always his son. If the "guardian" is a son, what could the "day of pollination"―a day when palm trees procreate―stand for? With these questions in mind, the di-xílíyə meaning is revealed:

God, even though I have used all my strength in sexual intercourse with my wife, I still have no son. Please give me a son.

This final example of veiled language is even more complex.

ˀinem taˁtubur mosḥit

di-idher bə-fəgə̄no

ḵəṣəho de ol ḵerʌṣ́ʌ

ol ḥʌṭmemhiyon di-ġɔ̄wṣ́ef

Why are you looking at mosḥit grass,

You who live in the bare, infertile wastes?

That fresh green grass is not the wretched ḵorṣ́ʌ grass

Of the ḥʌṭmemhiyon ġɔ̄wṣ́ef.

The Soqotri words here mean, on face value,

  • mosḥit : A tall, green grass good for goats to eat
  • Wretched ḵorṣ́ʌ : The word the poet uses is ḵerʌṣ́ʌ, the derogatory form of the neutral word ḵorṣ́ʌ. Ḵorṣ́ʌ is a type of grass that grows on salty and infertile land and is bad for goats to graze; it is usually given to sheep, which are considered inferior to goats in Soqotri culture.
  • ḥʌṭmemhiyon : This word derives from the verb stem ḥ-ṭ-m-m with the meaning of "dried-out; shriveled; brittle".
  • ġɔ̄wṣ́ef : A small depression in the ground.

A loose translation of the face-value meaning might be:

Why are you looking at good green grass when you live in a hole in a dry, brittle land? That's not the grass that suits where you're from.

But there are a few things to note that help discern the veiled meaning:

  • Mosḥit is an inland mountain grass, while ḵorṣ́ʌ is a coastal grass. Grass can be a metaphor for women.
  • The word ḥʌṭmemhiyon has the double meaning of the coastal plain of Socotra.
  • The word ġɔ̄wṣ́ef can also refer to the island's coastal villages.

The poem is actually a derogatory jab at a man from the coast. The speaker is a person of the mountains, and the grass is metaphor for the women of the mountains and coast respectively:

Why are you looking at mountain women when you live in a hole-like slum on the shriveled coastal plain? Our women are much too good for men like you, they aren't like the ḵorṣ́ʌ-like women on the coast.
