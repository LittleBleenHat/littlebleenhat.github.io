Genres: ʿadar

SAHO

  • Affiliation: Afro-Asiatic > Cushitic > East Cushitic > Saho
  • Area: Nearly 300,000 speakers, mostly in the central Eritrean coastline
  • ISO 639-3 code: ssy

The Saho are a Muslim Cushitic people, traditionally pastoralists, who make up approximately 5% of the population of the East African nation of Eritrea. The language they speak exists in a dialect continuum with Afar, another Cushitic language with ten times as many speakers as Saho, of which some linguists classify Saho as a mere dialect.

The Saho have three traditional genres of poetry. The first, ʿadar, ; these are respected works by known authors with artistic intent. The second genre consists of anonymous and sung folk poetry, such as wedding songs and work songs. The final genre comprises religious hymns sung by Sufis, purveyors of the mystical side of Islam, called nazme (cf. Arabic naẓm "versification").

Eritrea is one of the most authoritarian nations on earth, and Saho poetry consequently remains very understudied by outsiders. A single ʿadar about an 1890s proxy war between Italy and an Ethiopian prince, and two nazme, have been translated by French scholar Didier Morin and Italians Giorgio Banti and Moreno Vergari respectively. But that seems to be the virtual limit of foreign scholarship on the subject.


Organizing methods

Saho meter is syllable-counting, although I have not been able to identify the full list of variants. There appear to be at least two: a twelve-syllable variant with a caesura (fixed word boundary), and a nine-syllable variant without a caesura. The two translated examples of nazme are both examples of the former, with a caesura between the sixth and seventh syllables. Translation is revised from Banti & Vergari 2014 to better align with Saho word order.

Qabr-ak-an tiyadde, adox add-ullusa;

Lamma ak yayyaaʿe, tiy-adde raaʿisha.

ʿamali yoomenkoo, igirh kee tsariisa;

ʿamali yemʿenkoo, qabr-akah ifisha.

In what they call the grave, three He sends down;

Two He brings out, one He leaves inside.

If his deeds were bad, scorpions and snakes;

If his deeds were good, He illuminates the tomb for him.

In this nazme, the poet intentionally elides vowels to fit the twelve-syllable meter, which I mark by hyphens. The first line, for example, would normally have been pronounced "Qabre ak yan tiyadde, adox adde ullusa". The opposite can happen too. Sometimes a line is too short, and the poet adds dummy syllables like ha or hi to expand the line:

ʿumri nok bakiteh, mango xazan lino;

Qafla ni raddeh ha, mango zunuub lino;

Rabbi raxiim kinnih, Kayyad raja lino.

ʿaddada mā kāna wamā yakūnu.

Since our age has come to end, we have a great sorrow;

Since heedlessness has fallen upon us, we have many sins;

Since the Lord is merciful, in Him we have hope.

[In Arabic] He told everything that has been and will be.

"Nazme Stanza 3", Soliiman Ismaaʿil Maxammad, 1970s

Morin's ʿadar has a steady nine-syllable meter, but without a consistent caesura rule, as seen in the following stanza:

ʿagisen:

"Af-Gira ʿangita ʿangita;

"Xaga alayto bukkussa ee,

"Dadaʿ farena ise-l hayta ee.

"Ala-kkah aa dhiʿ ellih lino;

"Numa-kkah aa midhe ed lino?"

Others added:

"Af-Gira, victims of your enemies;

"Summer, you gnaw on stale bread,

"Winter, you die of thirst.

"You are but goatherds, with no interest for us;

"Apart from women, what penis do we find on you?"

Although not marked by Morin, I think it likely that farena ise-l was contracted to faren-ise-l to keep the nine-syllable beat.

As is clear from the nazme examples, Saho poetry features rhyme extensively. The most common form of rhyme is AAAA, when the entire stanza shares the same rhyme. Unlike English, Saho seems to prefer feminine rhyme, which begins from the vowel of the penultimate syllable (in this excerpt of Morin, discussing an old man's warnings about war, -amaa):

Ya-ʿammi ʿAbdalla fanad rade:

"Wesen yi nangalok ak tamaa:

"Lamma fatre-mmadhig obtamaa

"Sin-lih ma nadhige gab tamaa

"Dat-ʿaleyta-d gira haytamaa

"Makʿannile gira urussamaa

"Xane male-mmadhig rabtamaa

"Katay male-mmadhig beytamaa

"Gaba dabbin nexe daktamaa."

Ye ʿAbdalla.

My uncle ʿAbdalla interrupted:

"Say this to my cousin Wesen:

"We know that these were two months of war

"We know that with you, this cannot stand

"That you have burned Dat-ʿaleyta down

"That you have set Makʿannile on fire

"That nothing can avenge the dead

"That what has been taken cannot be retaken

"That the orphan cannot fight back."

Said ʿAbdalla.

Unlike English and many European languages, Saho also permits the same word to rhyme with itself, as happens with lino "we have" in the third stanza of Soliiman's nazme quoted above.

Alliteration and assonance are also common, though they appear unsystematic. In the first stanza of Soliiman's nazme, we find alliteration in and between the first two lines and assonance between the first and third lines at the beginning and end. In the second stanza, the major verbs (yiqhfile "was careless", yimlike "prevailed", yaʿiddibe "torments", yiqqirribe "is nearby") are integrated by alliteration and assonance (first stanza from Banti & Vergari 2014, second stanza revised by me to make chiasm in original Saho text clearer):

Yi Rabbi yol raxmat, Ku raxma waasiʿa

Sugto lem sorhah, inni nabseh weeʿa

Yi taʿizibbe ged, Kok xino marhiiʿa.

ʿaddada mā kāna wamā yakūnu.


Yi nabsi yiqhfile, yimlike zunuube

Ishe miyaarhige, mara yaʿiddibe

Yekkeleh mayane, kafan yiqqirribe

ʿaddada mā kāna wamā yakūnu.

My Lord, be merciful with me, Your mercy is huge

Since I don‘t know what awaits me, I cry for myself

If You punish me, I can‘t rebel against You.

[In Arabic] He told everything that has been and will be.


My soul has been careless, prevail did my sins;

It knows not itself, and my fellows it harms;

It has not realized the shroud is nearby.

[In Arabic] He told everything that has been and will be.

"Nazme Stanzas 1 and 2", Soliiman Ismaaʿil Maxammad, 1970s

Syntactic parallelism is also common in Saho poetry, as several of the stanzas quoted above easily demonstrate.


Genres: ʿadar

As the Introduction states, there are three traditional genres of Saho poetry. Of the three, the work of the ʿadarena (ʿadar writers) fits the Western archetype of the poet best; unlike the largely anonymous folk songs, or the mystical undertones of nazme, ʿadar are composed by individual ʿadarenas who take to the pen with serious purposes in mind and who are respected for their art.

In the closely related Afar, ʿadar means simply "song", but in Saho, the word refers to a specific genre of poetry that demands that most of the poem must be words directly spoken by the characters (properly metered and rhymed, of course). Every now and then the speech is interrupted by phrases like "they said" or "they were at such-and-such place". These do not fit either the meter or the rhyme and serve both to divide the text into manageable blocks and set the time and place for the direct quotations that make up the meat of the work.

The following, from Morin's 1890s-proxy-war ʿadar, is a good example. The Ethiopian prince Ras Sibxat threatens the Italian-affiliated Saho clans. The clansmen are unfazed—but their chief ʿOsman seems rather reluctant. (my translation from Morin 1995's French; blatta and shum are noble titles)

"Kela Tilyaani sinah marta,

"Nabam rabta-do ʿindham ʿarta."

Ye Ras Sibxat.


[......]


Biletta ʿUsbanak yen biletta ʿUsbanak,

"Xadisil murkanih xuggusa,

"Dudubul murkani xuggusa,

"Yinʿibem fitna muguugusa,

"Xarer ʿare-dd-anii kummusa,

"Dahab ʿare-dd-anii kummusa."

Ak en.


"Dadaʿnih iyyi ni dadʿishoo?

"Furusni iyyi ni fursuusoo?

"Teleyye shum ʿAli hin kisho."

Ye shum ʿOsman.


"Shum ʿOsman naba tiya kuuk yine,

"Shum ʿAli kishoh muʿuk yine,

"Nanu nemeete sinim sitaa,

"Ruwaage ko akko daʿarsitaa,

"Laa-ko akko helelsitaa."

Yedhhe Ras Sibxat.


"Kotta xerat badha ilalsinna,

"Ruwaage-ko akk mi daʿarsinna,

"Laa-ko akk mi helelsinnaa."

Yenih mals akah yoxoyn.

"The Italians will not always help you,

"Though the oldest die, the young will rise up."

Said Ras Sibxat.


[......]


They said to the blatta ʿOsman, on the blatta ʿOsman,

"He multiplies the crops at Xadis,

"He multiplies the crops in the bush,

"Even when angry, he rouses no strife,

"He has silk in his house but he wears a simple loincloth,

"Has gold in his house but wears a simple loincloth,"

They repeated.


"When we flock toward the sea, who will protect us?

"When we go up again, who will protect us?

"Since shum ʿAli's death the tribe is much weakened,"

Said shum ʿOsman.


"Shum ʿOsman was very right,

"Shum ʿAli was good for your tribe,

"But behold; we will exterminate you,

"So squeeze out the last milk from your heifers,

"And count no more on your cows."

Said Ras Sibxat.


"You son of an ass! We await you, feet firm,

"We will not milk our heifers,"

"We will not bid our cows farewell."

Was the response of them all.
